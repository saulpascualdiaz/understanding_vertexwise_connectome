Understanding Vertex-wise Structural Connectomes

From streamlines to a 244 000 × 244 000 sparse matrix — what each step does, why it matters, and how to build one on your data.

1. What is a structural connectome?

Prerequisite reading — how DWI works

This tutorial assumes you already know what a diffusion-weighted image is and how tractography produces streamlines. If those terms are new, work through understanding_dwi first — same series, covers DWI acquisition, preprocessing, the diffusion tensor, CSD, and tractography. It is the upstream half of the pipeline that produces the inputs we use here.

A structural connectome is a matrix where each row and column is a brain location, and each cell holds the strength of the white-matter pathway connecting them. The values come from diffusion-MRI tractography: we follow water's preferred direction of motion to reconstruct streamlines, and each streamline links the two cortical points where it terminates.

Two design choices determine what kind of connectome you get:

  1. The nodes. A small atlas like Desikan-Killiany gives a 68×68 matrix — one cell per pair of regions. A high-resolution cortical surface gives a ~244 000 × 244 000 matrix — one cell per pair of cortical vertices.
  2. The edge weight. Raw streamline counts? SIFT2-weighted counts? Mean FA along the streamline? Each choice probes a different aspect of the underlying white matter.

This tutorial is about the vertex-wise connectome, the highest-resolution option, where the nodes are individual points on a FreeSurfer cortical surface and the edges are SIFT2-weighted streamline densities distributed across the top-k nearest vertices.

Why vertex-wise, when ROIs are easier?

An ROI matrix averages thousands of vertices into a single cell. That averaging is convenient for group statistics but destructive for subject-specific patterns. A 5×3 cm parcel labelled "lateral temporal cortex" might be a single functional unit in one person and four separate sub-systems in another. Vertex-wise preserves both possibilities. The Gordon lab's precision-imaging programme (Gordon et al., Neuron 2017; Nature 2023) showed for resting-state fMRI that individuals deviate substantially from any group parcellation; for structural connectivity the same is true.

What you are looking at — the validation cohort

Every empirical number, screenshot, and figure in this tutorial comes from validation on the MICA-MICs open dataset (Royer et al., Scientific Data 2022 — the “CON” healthy-control arm, n = 20 adults, multi-shell DWI, 5 M streamlines per subject after MRtrix3 ACT + SIFT2). The pipeline itself is general — numbers you obtain on your own data will differ. We chose MICA because its multi-shell DWI is publicly released under a FAIR-compliant licence, so every result quoted here is reproducible.

2. The subject-based approach — why precision imaging?

The classical neuroimaging study works like this: pick an atlas, parcellate every subject the same way, average their connectivity matrices, compare groups. It works because the law of large numbers smooths over individual variation. But the cost is hidden: every interesting subject-specific signal has been averaged away before you ever ran your test.

The precision-imaging framing — pushed hard by Gordon, Petersen, and Laumann in resting-state fMRI — flips that. Instead of asking "how does the average network differ between groups", ask "what does this person's network look like, and how does it differ from another person's network?" Then the group is a distribution of individual networks, not a smoothed template.

Three concrete things you can do once your unit of analysis is a single subject's connectome:

  1. Fingerprinting / identifiability. Each subject's connectome is a personal signature. Pairwise Pearson correlation across the matrix: the self-pair (the same subject across two sessions, or L0 vs degraded L1) should score higher than any other-subject pair. The Amico-Goñi Idiff = mean(self r) − mean(other r) quantifies this. On the MICA test cohort (n = 19), vertex-wise connectomes give Idiff ≈ 0.46 vs 0.14 for DK-68 atlas matrices computed from the same subjects — roughly a 3× gain in identifiability (see figure below).
  2. Subject-specific communities. Running Louvain or Infomap on each subject's matrix returns a partition that is their partition — module boundaries shift, module counts vary (12 to 25 in our cohort), some chains exist for one person and not for another. Group claims are made by aligning these subject-specific partitions, not by imposing a template.
  3. Variant maps. For each vertex, compute how much that subject's connectivity profile deviates from a cohort reference. Repeat per subject. You get a per-subject "where am I unusual" map — the structural analogue of Gordon's functional variants.
Idiff = mean(self-pair Pearson r) − mean(other-pair Pearson r), computed on a 19-subject test cohort. The DK-68 atlas connectome gives Idiff ≈ 0.14; the same subjects’ data-driven vertex-wise matrices give Idiff ≈ 0.46 — about 3.4× better identifiability. The averaging step in atlas-based pipelines is where most of the subject-specific signal is lost.

Subject-level and group-level — complementary, not competing

Subject-based analysis does not replace group-level inference; it adds a layer below it. Group statistics are still available by aggregating per-subject measurements (cohort-mean variant maps, group hub overlap, prevalence of chain-type modules). What you also gain is access to within-cohort variability that population averaging would have hidden: subjects can be grouped by their own connectome patterns rather than only by external diagnostic labels — which opens the possibility of discovering sub-phenotypes the original labels do not capture.

3. From ROIs to vertices

The choice of nodes determines what the matrix can tell you. Comparing the two extremes:

ROI matrix — 68 × 68

  • 4 624 cells total — one screenshot is enough to see it.
  • Cells average over thousands of streamlines — high SNR.
  • Group statistics are straightforward (each cell has plenty of power).
  • Module boundaries are fixed by the atlas, identical across subjects.
  • You cannot ask "where exactly inside lateral temporal does this subject's chain run?"

Vertex-wise matrix — 244 000 × 244 000 (sparse)

  • ~ 50 million non-zero edges (0.08 % density) — needs a sparse matrix.
  • Each cell averages over ~ 0.5 - 5 streamlines — low SNR per cell, recovered by aggregation.
  • Module boundaries are subject-specific — community detection produces a different partition for every subject.
  • Chain-like elongated communities along major fibre tracts become visible.
  • Requires careful quality control: low DWI quality breaks vertex-wise faster than it breaks ROIs.

The right answer depends on the question. For "are the two groups different on average", an ROI matrix is fine. For "what's unique about this subject" — clinically, individually — you need vertex resolution.

4. The four inputs you need

Per subject, four pieces of data go into the matrix-construction step. Each one has a specific role; drop any of them and you cannot produce a meaningful connectome.

Four inputs, one script, three artefacts.

(a) Tractogram tractogram.tck

A whole-brain tractogram generated upstream with MRtrix's tckgen, FSL's probtrackx, or similar. The MICA cohort uses 5 million streamlines per subject seeded from the grey/white interface with anatomically-constrained tractography (ACT). For this pipeline, the body of each streamline doesn't matter — we only ever use the two endpoints.

(b) SIFT2 weights sift2_weights.txt

One float per streamline. SIFT2 (Smith et al. 2015) calibrates streamline density to match the diffusion model's local fibre orientation distribution: without it, short streamlines are over-counted and the matrix is biased. The aggregation step is:

C[u, v]  =  ∑streamlines s   sift[s] · MA[s, u] · MB[s, v]
MA, MB are the Mahalanobis assignment weights of the two endpoints

The UNIT_WEIGHTS fallback

If the upstream tractogram was SIFT-filtered (streamlines removed) rather than SIFT-2-weighted, you don't have per-streamline weights. The pipeline accepts a one-line file with the string UNIT_WEIGHTS as a sentinel meaning "use 1.0 for every streamline". This is the typical situation for clinical data such as ADNI tractograms.

(c) FreeSurfer subject directory <FS_dir>/sub-XXX/

The output of recon-all on the subject's T1. The pipeline reads five specific files inside it:

The 4D series is not required for matrix construction — only the registration target. That's important for cluster deployments where shipping ~ 1 GB of 4D DWI per subject is expensive.

5. Endpoint → vertex assignment

This is the technical heart of the pipeline. Each streamline endpoint is a 3D point in DWI space. To turn it into a graph node it has to be associated with one or more cortical vertices. The naive answer — snap to the nearest vertex — loses too much: an endpoint sitting exactly between two vertices should arguably contribute to both.

Instead, we use a Mahalanobis multi-vertex assignment. For every vertex within a search ball, we compute a directional distance:

d2(endpoint, v)  =  (Δn / σn)2  +  (Δt / σt)2
Δn = offset along the vertex normal · Δt = offset tangential to the surface

The endpoint then contributes weight exp(−d2/2) to its top-k nearest vertices, with the cutoff that d ≤ ball × σ. Weights are normalised so each streamline contributes a total mass of 1 (per endpoint).

Why a directional metric?

Anisotropic uncertainty makes physical sense

Streamlines reach the cortex from below the surface, through the white matter. A small offset along the inward-pointing normal direction just means "this streamline ended a millimetre deeper than the surface mesh" — an expected tractography artefact. A small tangential offset means "this streamline arrived at a different cortical location" — that should change the assignment. So the metric should be more forgiving in the normal direction.

Defaults: σn = 2 mm, σt = 2 mm gives roughly isotropic behaviour, but you can decouple them.

Interactive: see how weight depends on the parameters

d² = (Δn/σn)² + (Δt/σt)² = 1.31   →   weight = exp(−d²/2) = 0.519
A weight near 1 means strong contribution; near 0 means none.

What one row means

The row C[u, :] is vertex u's connectivity profile — how strongly it's linked to every other vertex. For a left precentral-gyrus vertex you'd expect:

  • A few hundred non-zero entries
  • The strongest weights on neighbouring vertices on the same gyrus (short-range U-fibres)
  • A small set of long-range weights into homotopic right-hemisphere motor cortex (callosal connections), and along the cortico-spinal pathway down into the spinal motor map
  • Zero almost everywhere else — that's why the matrix is sparse

Coverage — the single number you should always check

coverage_pct in meta.json is the percentage of cortical vertices with at least one streamline endpoint within the assignment ball. It's a one-line summary of how well the tractogram + registration combination managed to fill the cortex.

What good coverage looks like

The MICA-MICs CON cohort has coverage between 77 % (lowest subject) and 93.5 % (highest), median ~ 85 %. At that level, subject-specific community structure is recoverable and reproducible.

What bad coverage looks like

Below 80 % coverage, large portions of the cortex are missing edges and downstream analyses (in particular community detection) become unstable. Coverage above 85 % is what you should be aiming for on research-grade DWI.

7. Reading the matrix: communities

Given the matrix, the first question you can ask is: what are this subject's network modules? Two algorithms answer at different scales.

Louvain — macroscale

Greedy modularity-maximisation. Finds 12 - 25 large communities per subject, each covering several thousand vertices. Recovers the "big" systems: sensorimotor, ventral visual, default-mode.

~ 2 min/subject igraph (C backend) resolution-limited

Infomap — mesoscale

Information-theoretic random-walk compression. Finds hundreds of small modules per density level; after min_community_size = 3000 filtering, settles around 20 - 30. Reveals elongated chains that Louvain merges together.

~ 10 min/subject scale-free handles fragments

(A) For every subject (n = 20), the fraction of each Infomap module that lies inside a single Louvain module — containment ≈ 0.91 across the cohort. (B) A single subject's Louvain × Infomap confusion matrix: the block-diagonal shows Infomap split each Louvain macro-network into 1 - 3 finer sub-modules. (C) Min-community-size sweep: as you raise the floor, Infomap's module count collapses toward Louvain's. The two algorithms are observing the same hierarchy at different scales.

Why run both?

They answer different questions. Louvain gives you the canonical "which networks does this subject have"; Infomap gives you fine-grained sub-modules along major fibre tracts. Empirically the Infomap modules nest inside Louvain modules (containment ≈ 0.91 on MICA data) — the two algorithms are complementary, not redundant.

8. Reading the matrix: hubs

Given a partition, two classical graph metrics quantify how "central" each vertex is.

PCi  =  1 − ∑m (ki,m / ki)2
Participation coefficient — high when edges spread evenly across many modules (connector hub)
WMzi  =  (ki,m(i) − <km(i)>) / σ(km(i))
Within-module z-score — how much higher this vertex's within-module degree is than its module's average
Top-5 % mean-strength hub vertices for one MICA subject, painted on the white surface (red = hub, grey = no signal / outside DWI FOV). Hubs concentrate in posterior cingulate, lateral temporal, and supramarginal regions — the same anatomical landmarks classical ROI rich-club studies identify, but here at vertex resolution (one dot = one cortical vertex, not one parcel).

The composite hub_score = PC × WMz identifies connector hubs: vertices that are central to their own module and bridge to many others. Hub maps differ substantially across subjects — another piece of the precision-imaging argument.

10. Quality control — when can you trust the matrix?

Some failure modes show up loudly (the script crashes). Others are silent: the connectome looks fine on standard metrics but the downstream analysis is unreliable. Knowing the catalogue up front saves you from publishing on broken data.

Failure mode 1 — Low coverage

Symptom. coverage_pct < 80. More than 20 % of cortical vertices received no streamlines.

Consequence. Subject-specific Louvain partitions collapse under noise. In our cohort, the lowest-coverage MICA subject (77 %) had partition ARI = 0.20 under a 50 % streamline-density perturbation; all other subjects (≥ 82 %) had ARI ≥ 0.66.

Decision. Drop subjects below 80 % for community-based analyses. They may still be usable for variant maps or chain analysis — lens-specific QC.

Failure mode 2 — The "refugee module"

Symptom. One Infomap module covers 30 - 40 % of the brain.

Consequence. That module is meaningless — it's where Infomap dumped unconnected and weakly-connected vertices. Hub statistics inside it are nonsense.

Fix. Either re-run Infomap with min_community_size adapted to coverage, or pre-mask unconnected vertices before clustering.

Failure mode 3 — High Q but fragile

Symptom. Modularity Q = 0.80, n_modules normal, coverage normal. But re-running on a 50 %-degraded version gives ARI < 0.5.

Consequence. The partition has razor-thin module boundaries that re-route under any perturbation. Nothing in standard L0 QC flags this.

Detection. A per-subject mini-degradation test (drop 50 % of streamlines, re-cluster, ARI vs L0) is the only reliable detector.

Failure mode 4 — Bad T1 ↔ DWI registration

Symptom. ep_d_median_mm > 2 for the chosen registration. Endpoints land in the wrong cortical patches.

Detection. The pipeline runs both BBR and FLIRT-MI and chooses the one with the smaller endpoint-to-surface median. Check that number in meta.json.

Minimum data quality — what the upstream DWI must provide

Two layers of "minimum" matter, and they are independent:

  1. DWI acquisition — what your scanner must collect for tractography to be reliable in the first place. Determined by the angular resolution and shell coverage of the diffusion sampling.
  2. Tractogram size after SIFT — given good DWI, how many streamlines you need to keep so the connectome is reproducible. We measured this on MICA (below).

What this means in practice

  • Clinical legacy DWI (single shell, b = 1000, 30 directions) — you can do single-tensor DTI and very rough tractography, but MSMT-CSD will fail. Vertex-wise connectomes built on this input will be noisy and lacking long-range fibres. not recommended

  • Research-grade DWI (3 shells, b ≈ 1000 / 2000 / 3000, 60 + dir per shell, ≤ 2 mm voxels — the MICA recipe) — full pipeline works as designed. recommended

11. From the matrix to your study

Once you have connectome.npz + anatomy.npz + meta.json per subject, every downstream analysis is just code:

  • Identifiability / fingerprinting — pairwise Pearson r on flattened upper triangles. Self-pair should win.
  • Community detection — Louvain (igraph) and Infomap on a thresholded version of the matrix.
  • Hub maps — participation coefficient, within-module z on the partition.
  • Gradient embeddings — diffusion-map decomposition (BrainSpace), projected to fsaverage, hyperaligned to a cohort basis. Variants = subject deviation from the basis.
  • Chain analysis — PCA elongation on Infomap modules.
  • Group statistics — permutation tests across the per-subject bundle, vertex by vertex.

Deployment shape

For a large cohort (e.g. ADNI, n = 300), the cluster job is just s01 per subject (~ 10 min). All downstream analyses run locally on the returned matrices — faster iteration, simpler cluster dependencies, smaller download footprint. The Tool tab generates the s01 command tailored to your paths and parameters.

#!/usr/bin/env python3
"""
v03/s01_subject_connectome.py
==============================
Step 1 of the v03 pipeline: build a per-subject vertex-wise structural
connectome.

Locked methodology (see PLAN.md and documentation/v03/s01_subject_connectome.md):

    1. T1 → DWI registration: per-subject hybrid.
       - Compute fresh BBR via FSL `epi_reg` (unless --skip-bbr is given).
       - Compute fresh FLIRT-MI as a fallback / comparison.
       - Choose the registration that minimises endpoint→surface median.
    2. Project FreeSurfer lh/rh.white surfaces to DWI space with the chosen
       transform. Save as .surf.gii.
    3. Vertex parcel labels: surface aparc.annot (registration-independent,
       gold-standard FreeSurfer DK).
    4. Endpoint → vertex assignment: Mahalanobis multi-vertex
       (σ_n=2 mm, σ_t=2 mm, top_k=5, ball=2σ). Each endpoint contributes
       to its top-k Mahalanobis-nearest vertices, weighted by exp(-d²/2).
    5. Connectome aggregation: C[u,v] = Σ_streamlines  sift[s]·M_A[s,u]·M_B[s,v],
       symmetrised. Stored sparse.
    6. Anatomy.npz: thickness + area (FreeSurfer surface morph) + FA at
       new vertex positions.
    7. meta.json: subject id, n_lh/n_total, registration choice and
       endpoint→surface diagnostic, parameters.

def _run(cmd, log: Path = None):
    r = subprocess.run([str(c) for c in cmd], capture_output=True, text=True)
    if log is not None:
        with open(log, "a") as f:
            f.write(f"\n$ {' '.join(map(str, cmd))}\n{r.stdout}{r.stderr}")
    if r.returncode != 0:
        raise RuntimeError(f"{cmd[0]} failed: {r.stderr[-2000:]}")


def _compute_bbr(b0: Path, t1: Path, work: Path, log: Path) -> Path | None:
    """Run epi_reg + invert + transformconvert. Returns the MRtrix .txt path,
    or None if BBR failed (e.g. no boundary points)."""
    out_root = work / "b0_to_t1"
    try:
        _run(["epi_reg", f"--epi={b0}", f"--t1={t1}", f"--t1brain={t1}",
              f"--out={out_root}"], log)
    except RuntimeError:
        return None
    bbr_mat = work / "b0_to_t1.mat"
    if not bbr_mat.exists():
        return None
    inv_mat = work / "t1_to_b0.mat"
    _run(["convert_xfm", "-omat", inv_mat, "-inverse", bbr_mat], log)
    bbr_txt = work / "t1_to_dwi_bbr.txt"
    _run(["transformconvert", inv_mat, t1, b0, "flirt_import", bbr_txt,
          "-force", "-quiet"], log)
    return bbr_txt


def _compute_flirt_mi(b0: Path, t1: Path, work: Path, log: Path) -> Path:
    init_mat = work / "init.mat"
    _run(["flirt", "-in", t1, "-ref", b0,
          "-cost", "mutualinfo", "-dof", "6",
          "-omat", init_mat,
          "-out", work / "T1_to_b0_init.nii.gz"], log)
    flirt_txt = work / "t1_to_dwi_flirt.txt"
    _run(["transformconvert", init_mat, t1, b0, "flirt_import", flirt_txt,
          "-force", "-quiet"], log)
    return flirt_txt


def _ep_d_median(coords_native_lh, coords_native_rh, tk2s, t_d, eps_sample):
    """Endpoint→surface distance median using a candidate transform."""
    lh_dwi = transform_to_dwi(coords_native_lh, tk2s, t_d)
    rh_dwi = transform_to_dwi(coords_native_rh, tk2s, t_d)
    tree = cKDTree(np.vstack([lh_dwi, rh_dwi]))
    d, _ = tree.query(eps_sample)
    return float(np.median(d)), lh_dwi, rh_dwi

#  Main per-subject pipeline

def process_subject(subject: str, out_dir: Path,
                     dwi_dir: Path, fs_dir: Path, tract_dir: Path,
                     sigma_n: float = 2.0, sigma_t: float = 2.0,
                     ball: float = 2.0, top_k: int = 5,
                     skip_bbr: bool = False,
                     keep_intermediate: bool = False) -> dict:
    out_dir.mkdir(parents=True, exist_ok=True)
    log = out_dir / "epi_reg.log"
    print(f"\n{'='*60}\n  {subject}\n{'='*60}", flush=True)

    fs = fs_dir / subject
    track = tract_dir / subject
    dwi  = dwi_dir / subject / "dwi_preprocessed.mif"
    if not dwi.exists():
        raise FileNotFoundError(f"missing DWI: {dwi}")

    # ─── Step 1.0: prepare b0 + T1 ────────────────────────────────────
    # Detect whether DWI is 4D (MICA: full series, need dwiextract+mean) or
    # 3D (ADNI: file is already a mean-b0 placeholder, skip extraction).
    print("[1.0] mean b0 + T1.nii.gz", flush=True)
    b0 = out_dir / "_b0.nii.gz"
    t1 = out_dir / "_T1.nii.gz"
    ndim = subprocess.run(
        ["mrinfo", str(dwi), "-ndim"], capture_output=True, text=True
    ).stdout.strip()
    if ndim == "3":
        print(f"      DWI is 3D — treating as pre-computed mean-b0", flush=True)
        _run(["mrconvert", str(dwi), str(b0), "-force", "-quiet"], log)
    else:
        _run(["bash", "-c",
              f". $FSLDIR/etc/fslconf/fsl.sh && "
              f"dwiextract {dwi} - -bzero -force -quiet | "
              f"mrmath - mean {b0} -axis 3 -force -quiet"], log)
    _run(["mrconvert", str(fs / "mri/brain.mgz"), str(t1),
          "-force", "-quiet"], log)

    # ─── Step 1.1: registrations ──────────────────────────────────────
    print("[1.1] registration (BBR via epi_reg + fresh FLIRT-MI)", flush=True)
    bbr_txt = None if skip_bbr else _compute_bbr(b0, t1, out_dir, log)
    flirt_txt = _compute_flirt_mi(b0, t1, out_dir, log)

    # ─── Step 1.2: native surfaces + tk2s ─────────────────────────────
    print("[1.2] FreeSurfer surfaces + tk2s", flush=True)
    tk2s = freesurfer_tk2s(fs / "mri/brain.mgz")
    lh_v, lh_f = nib.freesurfer.read_geometry(str(fs / "surf/lh.white"))
    rh_v, rh_f = nib.freesurfer.read_geometry(str(fs / "surf/rh.white"))
    n_lh = lh_v.shape[0]; n_total = n_lh + rh_v.shape[0]

    # ─── Step 1.3: load tractogram (endpoints) ───────────────────────
    print("[1.3] loading tractogram", flush=True)
    tck = load_tck(str(track / "tractogram.tck"))
    ep_a = np.array([s[0]  for s in tck.streamlines], dtype=np.float64)
    ep_b = np.array([s[-1] for s in tck.streamlines], dtype=np.float64)
    n_s = len(ep_a)
    print(f"      {n_s:,} streamlines", flush=True)

    rng = np.random.default_rng(42)
    samp_idx = rng.choice(2*n_s, size=50_000, replace=False)
    eps_all = np.vstack([ep_a, ep_b])
    eps_samp = eps_all[samp_idx]

    # ─── Step 1.4: pick registration with smallest endpoint→surface ──
    print("[1.4] choose registration by endpoint→surface median", flush=True)
    candidates = {"FLIRT": flirt_txt}
    if bbr_txt is not None: candidates["BBR"] = bbr_txt

    eval_results = {}
    for tag, p in candidates.items():
        t_d = load_t1_to_dwi(p)
        # transform_to_dwi composes tk2s (tkRAS → scanner RAS) and
        # t_d (T1 RAS → DWI) internally; pass them separately.
        med, lh_dwi, rh_dwi = _ep_d_median(lh_v, rh_v, tk2s, t_d, eps_samp)
        eval_results[tag] = {"ep_d_med": med, "transform": t_d,
                              "lh_dwi": lh_dwi, "rh_dwi": rh_dwi}
        print(f"      {tag}  ep_d_median = {med:.3f}", flush=True)
    winner = min(eval_results, key=lambda k: eval_results[k]["ep_d_med"])
    chosen = eval_results[winner]
    print(f"      → using {winner} ({chosen['ep_d_med']:.3f} mm)", flush=True)

    # Save chosen transform + surfaces in DWI space
    np.savetxt(str(out_dir / "t1_to_dwi.txt"), chosen["transform"],
                header=f"v03/s01 chosen registration: {winner}", fmt="%.18g")
    save_surf_gii(chosen["lh_dwi"], lh_f, out_dir / "lh.white.surf.gii")
    save_surf_gii(chosen["rh_dwi"], rh_f, out_dir / "rh.white.surf.gii")

    # ─── Step 1.5: Mahalanobis multi-vertex assignment ────────────────
    print(f"[1.5] Mahalanobis multi-vertex (σ_n={sigma_n}, σ_t={sigma_t}, "
          f"top_k={top_k}, ball={ball}σ)", flush=True)
    vertices = np.vstack([chosen["lh_dwi"], chosen["rh_dwi"]])
    normals  = np.vstack([vertex_normals(chosen["lh_dwi"], lh_f),
                           vertex_normals(chosen["rh_dwi"], rh_f)])

    # SIFT2 weights — accept the "UNIT_WEIGHTS …" sentinel as a marker
    # that the tractogram has already been SIFT-filtered upstream and we
    # should use uniform weights of 1.0. Used by ADNI inputs where the
    # per-streamline weights aren't available but the tractogram has been
    # filtered to N M streamlines via tcksift (filtering, not tcksift2).
    txt = (track / "sift2_weights.txt").read_text()
    if txt.lstrip().startswith("UNIT_WEIGHTS"):
        print(f"      sift2_weights.txt = UNIT_WEIGHTS sentinel, "
              f"using uniform weights for {n_s:,} streamlines", flush=True)
        sift = np.ones(n_s, dtype=np.float32)
    else:
        lines = [l for l in txt.split("\n") if l.strip() and not l.startswith("#")]
        sift = np.fromstring(" ".join(lines), sep=" ").astype(np.float32)
        if len(sift) != n_s:
            raise ValueError(f"SIFT2 weights count {len(sift)} != streamlines {n_s}")

    t0 = time.time()
    ep_A, vtx_A, w_A = assign_mahalanobis(
        ep_a.astype(np.float32), vertices, normals,
        sigma_n=sigma_n, sigma_t=sigma_t, ball_threshold=ball, top_k=top_k)
    ep_B, vtx_B, w_B = assign_mahalanobis(
        ep_b.astype(np.float32), vertices, normals,
        sigma_n=sigma_n, sigma_t=sigma_t, ball_threshold=ball, top_k=top_k)
    print(f"      pairs A={len(vtx_A):,}  B={len(vtx_B):,}  in {time.time()-t0:.1f}s",
          flush=True)

    M_A = sp.coo_matrix((w_A, (ep_A, vtx_A)), shape=(n_s, n_total)).tocsr()
    M_B = sp.coo_matrix((w_B, (ep_B, vtx_B)), shape=(n_s, n_total)).tocsr()
    C = M_A.T @ sp.diags(sift) @ M_B
    C = (C + C.T).tocsr()
    sp.save_npz(str(out_dir / "connectome.npz"), C)

    deg = np.asarray(C.sum(axis=1)).ravel()
    n_conn = int((deg > 0).sum())
    cov = 100 * n_conn / n_total

    # ─── Step 1.6: anatomy.npz (per-vertex scalars + FS surfaces + parc) ──
    # Bundle everything downstream analyses need into one file so the cluster
    # only has to ship {connectome.npz, anatomy.npz, meta.json, lh/rh.white.surf.gii}
    # and every later step (community detection, hubs, hyperalignment to
    # fsaverage, chain analysis, parcel-level summaries) runs purely locally.
    print("[1.6] anatomy.npz", flush=True)
    # Per-vertex scalars (thickness, surface area, FA at the WM interface)
    lh_thk = nib.freesurfer.read_morph_data(str(fs / "surf/lh.thickness"))
    rh_thk = nib.freesurfer.read_morph_data(str(fs / "surf/rh.thickness"))
    lh_ar  = nib.freesurfer.read_morph_data(str(fs / "surf/lh.area"))
    rh_ar  = nib.freesurfer.read_morph_data(str(fs / "surf/rh.area"))
    fa_path = track / "fa.nii.gz"
    if fa_path.exists():
        fa_img = nib.load(str(fa_path))
        fa_data = fa_img.get_fdata(dtype=np.float32)
        inv = np.linalg.inv(fa_img.affine)
        vox = np.round((inv @ np.hstack([vertices, np.ones((n_total, 1))]).T)
                        .T[:, :3]).astype(np.int32)
        for ax in range(3):
            vox[:, ax] = np.clip(vox[:, ax], 0, fa_data.shape[ax] - 1)
        fa = fa_data[vox[:,0], vox[:,1], vox[:,2]].astype(np.float32)
    else:
        fa = np.zeros(n_total, dtype=np.float32)

    # FreeSurfer surfaces needed downstream: sphere.reg (fsaverage projection),
    # inflated (chain elongation / visualisation). White is already in
    # lh/rh.white.surf.gii in DWI space — we also store it here in native FS
    # coords for completeness.
    lh_sph_v, lh_sph_f = nib.freesurfer.read_geometry(str(fs / "surf/lh.sphere.reg"))
    rh_sph_v, rh_sph_f = nib.freesurfer.read_geometry(str(fs / "surf/rh.sphere.reg"))
    lh_inf_v, lh_inf_f = nib.freesurfer.read_geometry(str(fs / "surf/lh.inflated"))
    rh_inf_v, rh_inf_f = nib.freesurfer.read_geometry(str(fs / "surf/rh.inflated"))

    # DK parcellation labels per vertex + lookup names. We use the same
    # offset convention as _common.surface_aparc_labels: lh codes become
    # 1000 + code, rh codes become 2000 + code, so the concatenated array
    # is unambiguous across hemispheres. Code 0 (medial wall / unknown)
    # stays 0 in both. `aparc_names` maps the offset code → name.
    lh_ap, _, lh_names = nib.freesurfer.read_annot(str(fs / "label/lh.aparc.annot"))
    rh_ap, _, rh_names = nib.freesurfer.read_annot(str(fs / "label/rh.aparc.annot"))
    aparc = np.concatenate([
        np.where(lh_ap > 0, lh_ap + 1000, 0),
        np.where(rh_ap > 0, rh_ap + 2000, 0),
    ]).astype(np.int32)
    # Build a 35*2 + 1 lookup: 0 → "unknown", 1000+i → "lh.<name>", 2000+i → "rh.<name>"
    aparc_lut = {0: b"unknown"}
    for i, nm in enumerate(lh_names):
        aparc_lut[1000 + i] = b"lh." + (nm if isinstance(nm, bytes) else nm.encode())
    for i, nm in enumerate(rh_names):
        aparc_lut[2000 + i] = b"rh." + (nm if isinstance(nm, bytes) else nm.encode())
    # Save as two parallel arrays (codes, names) for npz portability
    aparc_codes = np.array(sorted(aparc_lut.keys()), dtype=np.int32)
    aparc_names = np.array([aparc_lut[c] for c in aparc_codes], dtype="O")

    np.savez_compressed(str(out_dir / "anatomy.npz"),
                         n_lh=np.int64(n_lh),
                         # per-vertex scalars
                         thickness=np.concatenate([lh_thk, rh_thk]).astype(np.float32),
                         area=np.concatenate([lh_ar,  rh_ar ]).astype(np.float32),
                         fa=fa,
                         # DK parcellation (codes use lh+1000 / rh+2000 offset)
                         aparc=aparc,
                         aparc_codes=aparc_codes,
                         aparc_names=aparc_names,
                         # FreeSurfer surfaces (native FS RAS coords)
                         lh_sphere_reg_v=lh_sph_v.astype(np.float32),
                         lh_sphere_reg_f=lh_sph_f.astype(np.int32),
                         rh_sphere_reg_v=rh_sph_v.astype(np.float32),
                         rh_sphere_reg_f=rh_sph_f.astype(np.int32),
                         lh_inflated_v=lh_inf_v.astype(np.float32),
                         lh_inflated_f=lh_inf_f.astype(np.int32),
                         rh_inflated_v=rh_inf_v.astype(np.float32),
                         rh_inflated_f=rh_inf_f.astype(np.int32))

    # ─── Step 1.7: meta.json ──────────────────────────────────────────
    write_json(out_dir / "meta.json", {
        "subject":              subject,
        "n_lh":                 int(n_lh),
        "n_total":              int(n_total),
        "registration_chosen":  winner,
        "registration_candidates": {k: float(v["ep_d_med"]) for k, v in eval_results.items()},
        "ep_d_median_mm":       float(chosen["ep_d_med"]),
        "coverage_pct":         float(cov),
        "n_connected":          int(n_conn),
        "nnz":                  int(C.nnz),
        "method":               "hybrid_registration + mahalanobis_multivertex",
        "params":               {"sigma_n_mm": sigma_n, "sigma_t_mm": sigma_t,
                                  "top_k": top_k, "ball_threshold_sigma": ball},
        "version":              "v03.s01",
    })

    print(f"\n[{subject}] coverage {cov:.1f}% ({n_conn:,}/{n_total:,})  "
          f"nnz={C.nnz:,}  (registration: {winner})", flush=True)

    # ─── Step 1.8: cleanup intermediates ─────────────────────────────
    if not keep_intermediate:
        for f in ["_b0.nii.gz", "_T1.nii.gz",
                   "init.mat", "T1_to_b0_init.nii.gz",
                   "b0_to_t1.mat", "b0_to_t1.nii.gz", "b0_to_t1_init.mat",
                   "b0_to_t1_fast_wmedge.nii.gz", "b0_to_t1_fast_wmseg.nii.gz",
                   "t1_to_b0.mat",
                   "t1_to_dwi_flirt.txt", "t1_to_dwi_bbr.txt"]:
            p = out_dir / f
            if p.exists(): p.unlink()

    return {"subject": subject, "registration": winner,
            "ep_d_median_mm": chosen["ep_d_med"],

            "n_connected": n_conn, "nnz": int(C.nnz)}

def launch_timestamp() -> str:

    return datetime.datetime.now().strftime("%Y%m%d_%H%M")

def freesurfer_tk2s(brain_mgz: Path) -> np.ndarray:
    """tkRAS → scanner RAS for a FreeSurfer brain.mgz."""
    img = nib.load(str(brain_mgz))
    return img.affine @ np.linalg.inv(img.header.get_vox2ras_tkr())


def transform_to_dwi(coords_native: np.ndarray, tk2s: np.ndarray,
                      t1_to_dwi: np.ndarray) -> np.ndarray:
    """tkRAS native → scanner RAS → DWI mm. Returns (n, 3) float32."""
    n = len(coords_native)
    c_ras = (tk2s @ np.hstack([coords_native, np.ones((n, 1))]).T).T[:, :3]
    full_T = t1_to_dwi
    c_dwi = (full_T @ np.hstack([c_ras, np.ones((n, 1))]).T).T[:, :3]
    return c_dwi.astype(np.float32)


def smooth_on_mesh(scalar: np.ndarray, faces: np.ndarray,
                    n_iter: int = 5, respect_nan: bool = True) -> np.ndarray:
    """
    Iterative neighbour-averaging on a triangular surface mesh.

    Approximates a small geodesic Gaussian. Each iteration replaces a vertex's
    value with `0.5·self + 0.5·mean(neighbours)`. With fsaverage spacing (~3 mm
    vertex pitch), N=5 iterations ≈ FWHM 3–4 mm; N=10 ≈ FWHM 5–7 mm.

    NaN-aware: NaN vertices stay NaN, and NaN neighbours are dropped from the
    averaging (do not propagate). Useful for masked maps (joint_valid,
    cortex-only). Output dtype matches input.

    O(n_iter · |E|) — a few seconds on 327k vertices for n_iter≤10.
    """
    n = scalar.shape[0]
    # Build adjacency once from face triplets
    nbrs = [set() for _ in range(n)]
    for (a, b, c) in faces:
        nbrs[int(a)].update((int(b), int(c)))
        nbrs[int(b)].update((int(a), int(c)))
        nbrs[int(c)].update((int(a), int(b)))
    nbrs = [np.fromiter(s, dtype=np.int64, count=len(s)) for s in nbrs]

    val = np.asarray(scalar, dtype=np.float64).copy()
    finite_init = np.isfinite(val) if respect_nan else np.ones(n, dtype=bool)
    for _ in range(n_iter):
        new = val.copy()
        for v in range(n):
            if not finite_init[v]:
                continue
            vals = val[nbrs[v]]
            ok = np.isfinite(vals)
            if not ok.any():
                continue
            new[v] = 0.5 * val[v] + 0.5 * vals[ok].mean()
        val = new
    return val.astype(scalar.dtype)


def vertex_normals(coords: np.ndarray, faces: np.ndarray) -> np.ndarray:
    """Per-vertex surface normal = normalized sum of adjacent face normals."""
    v0 = coords[faces[:, 0]]; v1 = coords[faces[:, 1]]; v2 = coords[faces[:, 2]]
    tn = np.cross(v1 - v0, v2 - v0)
    tn /= np.maximum(np.linalg.norm(tn, axis=1, keepdims=True), 1e-12)
    n = np.zeros_like(coords, dtype=np.float64)
    for axis in range(3):
        np.add.at(n, faces[:, axis], tn)
    n /= np.maximum(np.linalg.norm(n, axis=1, keepdims=True), 1e-12)
    return n.astype(np.float32)

def surface_aparc_labels(fs_dir: Path, n_lh: int, n_rh: int) -> np.ndarray:
    """
    Per-vertex DK parcel labels from FreeSurfer surface aparc.annot.
    Output uses the same 1000-1035 (lh) / 2000-2035 (rh) numbering as
    aparc+aseg.mgz, so endpoint volume-sampled labels can match this
    vertex labeling. Surface label 0 (medial wall / unknown) → 0.
    """
    lh, _, _ = nib.freesurfer.read_annot(str(fs_dir / "label/lh.aparc.annot"))
    rh, _, _ = nib.freesurfer.read_annot(str(fs_dir / "label/rh.aparc.annot"))
    if len(lh) != n_lh or len(rh) != n_rh:
        raise ValueError(
            f"aparc.annot count mismatch: lh.annot={len(lh)} (n_lh={n_lh}); "
            f"rh.annot={len(rh)} (n_rh={n_rh})")
    return np.concatenate([
        np.where(lh > 0, lh + 1000, 0),
        np.where(rh > 0, rh + 2000, 0),
    ]).astype(np.int32)


# ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
#  Mahalanobis multi-vertex endpoint assignment (anisotropic)
# ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

def assign_mahalanobis(endpoints: np.ndarray,
                        vertices: np.ndarray, normals: np.ndarray,
                        sigma_n: float = 2.0, sigma_t: float = 2.0,
                        ball_threshold: float = 2.0,
                        top_k: int = 5,
                        chunk: int = 200_000):
    """
    For each endpoint p, return up to `top_k` nearest vertices by Mahalanobis
    distance with anisotropic covariance:
        σ_n perpendicular to surface (penalizes off-surface offset)
        σ_t tangential (allows surface-aligned drift)
    Weights ∝ exp(-d² / 2), normalized so Σ weight = 1 per endpoint.

    Returns (ep_idx, vtx_idx, weight) flat arrays.
    """
    tree = cKDTree(vertices)
    eucl_radius = 3.0 * sigma_t
    out_ep, out_vtx, out_w = [], [], []

    for c0 in range(0, len(endpoints), chunk):
        c1 = min(c0 + chunk, len(endpoints))
        eps = endpoints[c0:c1]
        cand_lists = tree.query_ball_point(eps, r=eucl_radius)

        ep_local_list, vtx_flat_list, dM2_list = [], [], []
        for i, cands in enumerate(cand_lists):
            if not cands:
                continue
            cands = np.asarray(cands, dtype=np.int64)
            diffs = eps[i] - vertices[cands]
            nrmls = normals[cands]
            normal_comp = (diffs * nrmls).sum(axis=1)
            tangent_vec = diffs - normal_comp[:, None] * nrmls
            tangent_mag = np.linalg.norm(tangent_vec, axis=1)
            d_M2 = (normal_comp / sigma_n) ** 2 + (tangent_mag / sigma_t) ** 2
            within = d_M2 < (ball_threshold ** 2)
            if not within.any():
                continue
            cands = cands[within]; d_M2 = d_M2[within]
            if len(d_M2) > top_k:
                idx_sorted = np.argpartition(d_M2, top_k)[:top_k]
                cands = cands[idx_sorted]; d_M2 = d_M2[idx_sorted]
            ep_local_list.append(np.full(len(cands), i, dtype=np.int64))
            vtx_flat_list.append(cands)
            dM2_list.append(d_M2)

        if not ep_local_list:
            continue
        ep_local = np.concatenate(ep_local_list)
        vtx_flat = np.concatenate(vtx_flat_list)
        d_M2     = np.concatenate(dM2_list)
        w        = np.exp(-0.5 * d_M2).astype(np.float32)

        n_chunk = c1 - c0
        sum_per_ep = np.bincount(ep_local, weights=w, minlength=n_chunk)
        w = w / sum_per_ep[ep_local]

        out_ep.append(ep_local + c0)
        out_vtx.append(vtx_flat)
        out_w.append(w.astype(np.float32))

    if not out_ep:
        return (np.empty(0, dtype=np.int64), np.empty(0, dtype=np.int64),
                np.empty(0, dtype=np.float32))
    return (np.concatenate(out_ep), np.concatenate(out_vtx),
            np.concatenate(out_w))
